# Eval: Decision Utility Rubric

Use this rubric when you need to judge whether a report is actually useful for choosing, prioritizing, approving, rejecting, investing, partnering, or investigating further.

A report can be well sourced and reasonably deep yet still fail the real test: **does it help the reader decide what to do?**

## Goal

Measure decision usefulness separately from generic depth or factual plausibility.

This rubric is especially useful when a report feels smart but leaves the reader unsure what matters most, what to do next, or what could change the conclusion.

---

## Scoring scale

Use a 0-2 scale per dimension.

- **0** = weak or missing
- **1** = partly useful but not sharp enough
- **2** = clearly decision-useful

Suggested total for six dimensions:

- **0-4**: informative but low decision value
- **5-8**: partially decision-useful
- **9-12**: strong decision-support quality

---

## Dimension 1: Decision frame clarity

### What this measures
Whether the report identifies what judgment or decision the reader is trying to make.

### Look for
- clear statement of the practical question
- decision context rather than topic summary
- criteria that would change the final answer

### 0
- the report mostly explains the topic
- the actual decision frame is unclear

### 1
- a decision frame exists, but it is broad or weakly connected to the body

### 2
- the decision frame is explicit and drives the report structure

---

## Dimension 2: Load-bearing variable selection

### What this measures
Whether the report identifies the few variables that matter most instead of treating all facts as equally important.

### Look for
- 3-5 major variables prioritized
- clear explanation of why those variables matter more than the rest
- supporting detail subordinated to those variables

### 0
- many facts, little prioritization

### 1
- some prioritization exists, but too many variables feel equally weighted

### 2
- the report clearly identifies and explains the key drivers of the conclusion
- for market-outlook tasks, the report distinguishes drivers of change from blockers or frictions

---

## Dimension 3: Option or scenario usefulness

### What this measures
Whether the report helps compare choices, scenarios, or possible paths.

### Look for
- meaningful comparison between options
- scenario framing where appropriate
- explicit trade-offs, not just feature lists
- for market-outlook tasks, a base case plus at least one credible alternative scenario when uncertainty is material

### 0
- no real comparison or scenario usefulness

### 1
- some comparison exists, but trade-offs are weak or generic

### 2
- the report materially clarifies choices or scenarios

---

## Dimension 4: Conclusion sharpness

### What this measures
Whether the bottom line is clear enough to act on.

### Look for
- explicit bottom line
- recommendation, ranking, or directional judgment when appropriate
- not hiding behind endless caveats

### 0
- the report stays noncommittal even when evidence could support a judgment

### 1
- a conclusion exists, but it is soft, buried, or not operationally useful

### 2
- the report gives a clear and appropriately calibrated bottom line

---

## Dimension 5: Change-the-conclusion clarity

### What this measures
Whether the report explains what could overturn, weaken, or strengthen the conclusion.

### Look for
- explicit "what could change the view"
- critical assumptions called out
- next verification targets tied to uncertainty

### 0
- uncertainty exists, but its decision impact is unclear

### 1
- some uncertainty discussion exists, but it does not clearly connect to the judgment

### 2
- the report clearly states what would change the conclusion and why

---

## Dimension 6: Actionability of next steps

### What this measures
Whether the report tells the reader what to do next, verify next, or monitor next.

### Look for
- concrete next checks
- monitoring triggers
- sequencing or prioritization of follow-up actions
- stakeholder-specific actions when the report is meant for operators, buyers, builders, investors, or other distinct audiences

### 0
- no meaningful next step guidance

### 1
- some next steps exist, but they are generic or not prioritized

### 2
- the next steps are concrete, relevant, and logically derived from the analysis

---

## How to use this rubric

After reviewing a report:

1. score each dimension from 0 to 2
2. write one short note for each low score
3. identify whether the report is:
   - informative but not decision-useful
   - decision-oriented but weakly supported
   - strongly decision-useful
4. if the same weak dimension repeats across reports, create a new eval or update a checklist/template

---

## Interpreting patterns

### High depth, low decision utility
Likely symptoms:
- many facts and citations
- no clear prioritization
- bottom line too soft
- unclear what the reader should do

### Strong conclusion, weak utility discipline
Likely symptoms:
- decisive tone without enough explanation of drivers, assumptions, or change conditions

### Good utility, weak evidence
Likely symptoms:
- helpful-looking recommendation
- but confidence is overclaimed relative to evidence quality

---

## Suggested prompts

- Should we partner with this company, buy from it, or wait six months?
- Which of these three vendors is best for a mid-market deployment, and why?
- Is this market attractive enough for entry in the next 12 months?
- Should an investor treat this stock as a growth opportunity, a hold, or a avoid-for-now case?

---

## Final discipline

Before calling a report decision-useful, ask:

- Does it make the decision frame explicit?
- Does it identify what matters most?
- Does it clarify trade-offs or scenarios?
- Does it give a clear bottom line?
- Does it explain what could change the conclusion?
- Does it tell the reader what to do or verify next?

If several of these are weak, the report may still be informative — but it is not yet strong decision support.
