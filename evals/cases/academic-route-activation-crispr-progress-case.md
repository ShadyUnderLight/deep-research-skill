# Academic Route Activation Case: CRISPR Gene Editing Field Progress

## Case metadata

- **Case type**: Route activation validation
- **Route**: Academic / Literature Review (experimental)
- **Task type**: Academic field progress analysis
- **Date**: 2026-05-29
- **Status**: Validation case

## Task description

**User request**: "What is the current state of CRISPR gene editing research? Analyze the key breakthroughs, current challenges, and future directions based on recent academic literature."

**Expected route activation**: Academic / Literature Review (field progress analysis)

**Alternative route considered**: Market Outlook / Industry Evolution

**Why academic route wins**: The core question is about research progress, breakthroughs, and future directions in academic literature, not about commercial applications or market evolution.

## Route preflight analysis

### Primary route
- **Selected**: Academic / Literature Review
- **Reason**: Core question is about field progress analysis through academic literature

### Closest alternative route
- **Alternative**: Market Outlook / Industry Evolution
- **Why alternative loses**: The task focuses on research evidence and academic progress, not market adoption or commercial trajectory

### Opening must do
- Define scope of review (time period, sub-fields covered)
- Establish search strategy
- Identify key research themes
- Present evidence hierarchy

### Mandatory sections
- Search strategy and scope
- Key breakthroughs and milestones
- Current state of the art
- Open questions and challenges
- Future directions
- Evidence quality assessment

### Minimize / move later
- Commercial applications of CRISPR (belongs in market outlook)
- Company analysis of CRISPR startups (belongs in private company evaluation)
- Regulatory landscape (belongs in regulatory analysis)

### Visible proof the route fired
- Papers cited with publication type and peer-review status
- Evidence tier labels
- Explicit statement of search strategy
- Clear identification of breakthrough papers vs incremental work

### Hard-fail signs to watch
- Treating preprints as peer-reviewed
- Cherry-picking positive results
- Ignoring negative or conflicting findings
- Missing venue information

## Pass criteria

A good answer should:

### 1. Activate the correct route
- explicitly classify this as an academic field progress analysis
- not default to market outlook or generic research

### 2. Satisfy the artifact contract
- define search strategy and scope
- identify key breakthroughs with full citations
- describe current state of the art
- document open questions and future directions
- assess evidence quality with tier labels

### 3. Apply evidence hierarchy correctly
- label publication type and peer-review status for each paper
- distinguish study design quality from publication venue prestige
- respect discipline-specific venue prestige

### 4. Avoid hard-fail conditions
- no preprints treated as peer-reviewed
- no cherry-picking (include negative findings)
- no missing venue information
- no conflation of evidence dimensions

## Failure signs

The route activation failed if:

### Route selection failure
- the report is structured as a market outlook (focusing on commercial applications)
- the report focuses on company analysis instead of research progress
- the report is a generic overview without academic evidence assessment

### Artifact contract failure
- no search strategy documented
- no key breakthroughs identified
- no current state of the art described
- no evidence quality assessment

### Evidence hierarchy failure
- preprints cited without "not peer-reviewed" label
- no publication venue information
- evidence tiers not distinguished
- negative findings ignored

## Simulated evidence collection

### Search strategy
- **Databases**: PubMed, Google Scholar, bioRxiv
- **Search terms**: "CRISPR", "CRISPR-Cas9", "gene editing", "base editing", "prime editing"
- **Time range**: 2012-2024
- **Language**: English

### Key research themes

#### Theme 1: Foundational discoveries
1. **Jinek et al. (2012)** - "A Programmable Dual-RNA–Guided DNA Endonuclease in Adaptive Bacterial Immunity"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Science
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: Demonstrated programmable DNA cleavage with Cas9

2. **Cong et al. (2013)** - "Multiplex Genome Engineering Using CRISPR/Cas Systems"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Science
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: First demonstration of CRISPR-Cas9 for genome editing in mammalian cells

3. **Mali et al. (2013)** - "RNA-Guided Human Genome Engineering via Cas9"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Science
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: Independent confirmation of CRISPR-Cas9 in human cells

#### Theme 2: Precision improvements
4. **Komor et al. (2016)** - "Programmable editing of a target base in genomic DNA without double-stranded DNA cleavage"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Nature
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: Base editing (CBE), avoiding double-strand breaks

5. **Gaudelli et al. (2017)** - "Programmable base editing of A•T to G•C in genomic DNA without DNA cleavage"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Nature
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: Adenine base editor (ABE)

6. **Anzalone et al. (2019)** - "Search-and-replace genome editing without double-strand breaks or donor DNA"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Nature
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: Prime editing

#### Theme 3: Clinical applications
7. **Frangoul et al. (2021)** - "CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing for Sickle Cell Disease and β-Thalassemia"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] New England Journal of Medicine
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: First clinical evidence of CRISPR therapy efficacy

8. **Gillmore et al. (2021)** - "CRISPR-Cas9 In Vivo Gene Editing for Transthyretin Amyloidosis"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] New England Journal of Medicine
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: In vivo CRISPR editing in humans

#### Theme 4: Challenges and limitations
9. **Zhang et al. (2015)** - "Off-target cleavage assessment"
   - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Nature Biotechnology
   - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
   - Contribution: Systematic analysis of off-target effects

10. **Kosicki et al. (2018)** - "Repair of double-strand breaks induced by CRISPR–Cas9 leads to large deletions and complex rearrangements"
    - [Published, Peer-reviewed] Nature Biotechnology
    - Evidence tier: Tier 2 (peer-reviewed journal)
    - Contribution: Identified unintended large deletions

### Current state of the art
- Base editing and prime editing enable precise modifications without double-strand breaks
- Clinical trials show efficacy for sickle cell disease and transthyretin amyloidosis
- Ongoing challenges: off-target effects, delivery efficiency, immune responses
- Emerging: epigenome editing, RNA editing, CRISPR diagnostics

### Open questions and future directions
- How to improve delivery to specific tissues?
- How to minimize off-target effects?
- How to make CRISPR therapies more affordable?
- What are the long-term effects of germline editing?

## Lessons learned

### What worked well
- Field progress analysis was natural fit for academic route
- Evidence hierarchy helped distinguish foundational vs incremental work
- Theme-based organization improved readability

### What needs hardening
- Could add explicit "breakthrough vs incremental" classification
- Could add more structured timeline visualization
- Could add more explicit handling of conflicting findings

### Route-boundary observations
- Market outlook would have focused on commercial applications and market size
- Academic route better served the "research progress" framing
- Clear boundary: if user asked "what is the market size for CRISPR therapies?", market outlook would be correct
