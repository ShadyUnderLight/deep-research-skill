# Ranking and Current-Claims Discipline

Use this file when the research includes rankings, market position, "No.1" claims, category leadership, share statements, shipment/volume leadership, streak claims, or any other current-status statement whose meaning depends heavily on time window, source type, and category definition.

Do not treat ranking claims as simple facts. They are often narrow, time-sensitive, definition-sensitive, and source-sensitive.

## Core rule

Before using a strong current-position claim, make clear:

- what is being ranked
- who is ranking it
- what time window it covers
- what metric is being used
- whether the source is official, third-party, media-reported, or inferred

If these are not clear, reduce certainty and avoid strong leader-language.

## Why this matters

Claims like these can sound decisive while being quite fragile:

- `No.1 in China`
- `market leader`
- `top-ranked`
- `largest`
- `five straight months at No.1`
- `current market share is X%`

They may depend on:

- one narrow segment definition
- one data provider's taxonomy
- one month or quarter
- sell-in vs sell-through vs retail vs registration data
- company-selected framing rather than neutral comparison

## Claim checklist

Before using a ranking/current-position claim, ask:

- What exact category is this claim about?
- Is the category broad or narrow?
- What geography does it cover?
- What period does it cover?
- What metric is used: revenue, units, registrations, activations, shipments, deliveries?
- Who produced the ranking?
- Is the source primary, independent, media-reported, or company-quoted?
- Would a reader overgeneralize this claim if I phrase it too strongly?

If yes, rewrite it more carefully.

## Source hierarchy

For ranking/current-position claims, prefer:

1. direct primary disclosure with clear methodology
2. reputable independent market/research providers with defined categories and periods
3. reputable reporting that quotes a named source and defined metric
4. company marketing or media summaries only with clear caveats

Do not give company promotional phrasing the same weight as independent market statistics.

## Strong-claim rule

The stronger the wording, the stronger the evidence must be.

Examples:

- `meaningful presence` requires modest evidence
- `top-tier challenger` requires more evidence
- `among the leaders` requires still more
- `No.1` or `market leader` requires very strong, well-defined, time-stamped evidence

If the evidence is weaker, soften the wording.

## Good phrasing patterns

Prefer wording like:

- `According to [source], Xiaomi ranked first in [defined segment] during [period].`
- `Third-party data cited by [source] suggests ...`
- `In the specific [segment] and time window reviewed, ...`
- `This appears true for [defined category], but should not be generalized beyond it.`
- `Available market data indicates strong recent momentum rather than a stable long-term leadership position.`

## Weak phrasing patterns

Avoid:

- `X is the leader` without category/time/source context
- `X has been No.1` without saying where, in what metric, and for what period
- `current share is ...` when the data source or measurement basis is unclear
- mixing company-disclosed facts and external ranking claims under one undifferentiated confidence label

## Company facts vs market-position claims

Keep these separate:

### More stable / primary facts

- company-reported revenue
- official product pricing
- company-reported deliveries
- official store count
- official current product lineup

### More fragile / context-dependent claims

- share rank
- volume rank
- category leadership
- streak leadership
- current market position
- media-reported comparative standing

Do not collapse both into the same certainty bucket unless evidence quality really supports that.

## Time-sensitivity discipline

Ranking and current-position claims degrade quickly.

Be careful with:

- month-to-month leadership
- quarter-specific share changes
- launch-driven spikes
- newly created or selectively defined segments
- media reports that freeze one moment into a durable narrative

When useful, distinguish:

- short-term momentum
- recent period rank
- longer-term structural position

These are not the same thing.

## Decision-usefulness rule

Do not let flashy current claims dominate the report if they are not load-bearing for the decision.

Ask:

- Does this ranking actually change the judgment?
- Is this a durable signal or a narrow recent datapoint?
- Am I using this because it is relevant, or because it sounds impressive?

A report should prioritize robust decision variables over media-friendly statistics.

## Output expectation

When a ranking/current-position claim matters, the final report should make at least one of these visible:

- source type
- time basis
- metric basis
- category definition
- scope limitation

If that cannot be done clearly, use softer language or drop the claim.

## Red flags

Slow down and downgrade certainty when:

- the report wants to say `No.1` but the category is narrow or unclear
- the claim comes from media repetition rather than direct sourcing
- the metric basis is not obvious
- the period is short and volatile
- the statement sounds stronger than what the source can support
- the claim is being used to support a broad investment thesis without enough durability

## Final discipline

Before finalizing, ask:

- Is this an official fact, or a current-position claim?
- Did I define the category and period?
- Would a skeptical reader accuse this of overclaiming?
- Does this claim actually matter to the recommendation?

If in doubt, narrow the scope, soften the wording, or remove the claim.
