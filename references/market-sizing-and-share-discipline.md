# Market Sizing and Share Discipline

Use this file when the research involves market size, industry size, TAM/SAM/SOM, company share, competitive position, shipment share, revenue share, or any estimate that maps a company to a broader category.

Do not present market-size or market-share claims as if they are directly measurable when the mapping is weak.

## Core rule

Define the market before using the number.

A market-size figure is only useful if the report makes clear:

- what the market includes
- what it excludes
- what time period it covers
- what unit it uses
- whether the company data can actually be mapped into it

If those conditions are unclear, treat share estimates as rough directional judgments, not hard statistics.

## Separate these concepts

Do not blur together:

- total industry size
- served market size
- product-category size
- shipment share
- revenue share
- installed-base share
- procurement share
- training vs inference share
- domestic vs global share

Two numbers can sound compatible while referring to totally different scopes.

## Market definition checklist

Before using a market-size number, ask:

- What exactly is being measured?
- Is it revenue, shipments, procurement value, installed base, or another metric?
- What geography does it cover?
- What period does it cover?
- Which product classes are included?
- Which product classes are excluded?
- Does the company participate in that exact segment or only part of it?

If these are not clear, do not use the number as the basis for precise share claims.

## Mapping company data to market data

Do not assume company revenue maps cleanly into industry market size.

Check whether the company number includes:

- hardware only or hardware plus software/services
- system integration or cluster sales
- one segment or multiple segments
- domestic revenue or global revenue
- direct product sales or licensing/IP revenue

If the market number and company number are not on the same basis, any share estimate should be downgraded in confidence.

## Share-estimation rules

Use these rules when no directly auditable market-share data exists.

### Allowed with caution

- rough directional judgments
- broad share bands with explicit caveats
- relative position statements such as `leader`, `challenger`, `niche`, or `small but growing`
- segment-specific judgments when segment definition is clear

### Avoid unless strongly supported

- narrow percentage bands
- precise percentages
- visually authoritative tables that imply measurement certainty
- direct share calculations from mismatched market/company definitions

## Precision rule

The more specific the number, the higher the evidence bar.

Examples:

- `small share` requires relatively little evidence
- `low single-digit share` requires more evidence
- `5%–12% share` requires even more evidence
- `7.4% share` requires very strong direct evidence

If you do not have strong evidence, use less precise language.

## Good phrasing patterns

Prefer wording like:

- `publicly auditable market-share data appears limited`
- `the company likely has a small but non-trivial position in ...`
- `available evidence suggests it is a challenger rather than a leader`
- `any share estimate here should be treated as directional only`
- `this range is heuristic, not directly auditable`

## Weak phrasing patterns

Avoid wording like:

- `the company has 8%–15% share` when this is mainly inferred
- `the company controls X% of the market` without direct evidence
- tables that make rough guesses look like measured statistics

## Competitive-position fallback

When share data is weak, emphasize competitive position instead of pseudo-precise share.

Examples:

- ecosystem strength
- customer concentration
- visible deployments
- benchmark relevance
- channel access
- policy advantage/disadvantage
- product maturity
- segment fit

A robust relative-position judgment is often more honest than a shaky percentage.

## Time layering

Market-size and share claims are especially sensitive to timing.

Check:

- whether the market number is current or historical
- whether company revenue is from the same period
- whether category structure has shifted since the cited report
- whether a share estimate based on one year is being projected into another year without support

## Output expectation

If you include market-share discussion, make at least one of these explicit:

- the market definition
- the weakness of the mapping
- the directional nature of the estimate
- the missing data that prevents stronger precision

If confidence is low, prefer qualitative competitive-position analysis over fragile numeric share claims.

## Red flags

Slow down and downgrade confidence when:

- the market number comes from a generic industry report with unclear boundaries
- the company has mixed revenue streams that do not map cleanly to the cited market
- the share estimate relies mostly on company revenue and broad industry intuition
- the report is tempted to present a detailed share table without auditable underlying data
- segment boundaries like training/inference, domestic/global, or chip/card/system are not aligned

## Final discipline

Before finalizing, ask:

- Did I define the market clearly enough?
- Does the company number actually map to that market number?
- Am I claiming share, or just implying position?
- Is the precision level justified?
- Would a skeptical analyst accept this as disciplined, or reject it as pseudo-precision?

If the answer is unclear, reduce precision and increase caveats.
