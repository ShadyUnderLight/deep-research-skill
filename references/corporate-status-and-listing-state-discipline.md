# Corporate Status and Listing-State Discipline

Use this file when the research involves whether a company is private, filed for IPO, accepted for review, approved, registered, issued, listed, trading, delisted, merged, privatized, or otherwise in a changing capital-markets state.

Do not treat company status as static when the topic is time-sensitive. A stale status claim can distort the entire report.

## Core rule

Verify the company's current capital-markets state before using older financing, filing, or prospectus language as present-tense framing.

Do not anchor the report on one earlier milestone if the company may have moved to a later stage.

## Status ladder

Keep these states separate. They are not interchangeable.

- private / unlisted
- filed for IPO
- accepted for review
- under review
- passed review / hearing
- registered / approved to issue
- issued
- listed
- trading
- suspended
- delisted
- merged / acquired / privatized

Use the most current verified state available.

## Why this matters

A company's market status affects:

- valuation framing
- disclosure expectations
- source selection
- relevance of prospectus vs periodic filings
- how to interpret financing rounds and investor ownership

A report can appear detailed while being fundamentally misframed if it pins the company to the wrong status.

## Verification checklist

Before stating company status, ask:

- What is the company's current state as of the research date?
- Which source confirms this state?
- Is the source primary and current?
- Am I relying on a historical filing document for a present-tense claim?
- Has a later milestone superseded the earlier document I am citing?

If these are unclear, slow down and avoid strong current-state phrasing.

## Time layering

Separate:

- historical milestone
- current state
- expected next step

Examples:

- `The company filed for IPO in 2025.`
- `As of [date], it appears to remain under review.`
- `If approved, the next step would be ...`

Do not collapse all three into one vague present-tense sentence.

## Prospectus / filing discipline

Prospectuses, filing documents, and review materials are often essential sources, but they are time-bound.

Use them carefully:

- good for historical milestones, company description, disclosed risks, use-of-proceeds, earlier financial data
- not automatically sufficient for current listing state
- may become stale after later regulatory or market events

If you use a filing document, explicitly check whether a later listing-state change occurred.

## Financing-language discipline

Terms like these are especially prone to staleness:

- `Pre-IPO`
- `latest private valuation`
- `unicorn`
- `private round`
- `to-be-listed`

Do not use these as present-tense framing unless they still reflect the current state.

If the company has since listed, reframe them as historical milestones.

## Good phrasing patterns

Prefer:

- `According to [primary source], the company filed for IPO on ...`
- `As of [research date], the company appears to be ...`
- `The 2025 prospectus is used here as a historical filing source, not as proof of current trading status.`
- `The company completed a Pre-IPO round in 2024, but that no longer describes its current market status.`

## Weak phrasing patterns

Avoid:

- `the company is Pre-IPO` based only on an older filing
- `the company has filed, therefore it is effectively listed`
- `the company is listed` without exchange/ticker/trading context
- mixing historical IPO milestones and current status under one undifferentiated certainty label

## Evidence hierarchy

Prefer, in order:

1. current primary exchange/regulator/company disclosure about status
2. recent primary company disclosure that clearly states current status
3. reputable reporting quoting named primary sources
4. older filings used only as historical context, not as sole proof of current state

## If listed

If you conclude the company is listed, the report should usually make visible at least some of:

- exchange / market
- ticker or code
- listing date or current trading context
- what source confirms listed/trading status

## If not yet listed

If the company is still in process, say what stage it is actually in.

Examples:

- filed but not yet accepted
- accepted and under review
- approved but not yet issued
- issued but not yet trading

Be precise. `in IPO` is usually too vague.

## Red flags

Slow down and downgrade certainty when:

- the report relies heavily on a prospectus or filing from months earlier
- `Pre-IPO` language is still being used long after a major financing or regulatory milestone
- the report cannot cleanly tell whether the company is filed, approved, or trading
- capital-markets status is central to the thesis but not explicitly verified as current

## Final discipline

Before finalizing, ask:

- Did I verify the current company status as of the report date?
- Am I mistaking a historical milestone for the current state?
- Did I separate private/filed/listed/trading precisely enough?
- Would a skeptical reader say I froze the company at an older stage?

If in doubt, state the uncertainty and reduce confidence.
