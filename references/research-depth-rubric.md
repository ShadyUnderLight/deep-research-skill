# Research Depth Rubric

Use this file when you need to evaluate whether a report is merely factual-looking or actually demonstrates meaningful research depth and breadth.

Do not reduce research quality to simple fact-checking. A report can be factually plausible and still be shallow, narrow, or weakly analytical.

## Core idea

Evaluate depth across multiple dimensions, not with a single vague judgment.

A strong deep-research output should show:

- good framing
- sufficient coverage
- strong evidence discipline
- real analysis rather than summary
- serious counter-evidence handling
- decision usefulness

## Scoring scale

Use a simple 0-2 scale per dimension.

- **0** = clearly weak or missing
- **1** = partially present but uneven or limited
- **2** = strong and decision-useful

Suggested total for six dimensions:

- **0-4**: shallow
- **5-8**: moderate
- **9-12**: strong deep-research quality

Use the score directionally, not mechanically.

## Dimension 1: Question framing

### What this measures

Whether the report identifies the real question and decision context instead of only summarizing the surface topic.

### Look for

- a clear research objective
- decision context or user intent
- useful decomposition into subquestions
- prioritization of important questions over background trivia

### 0

- the report mostly summarizes the topic
- the real decision or judgment question is not clear

### 1

- some framing exists, but it is broad or generic
- subquestions exist, but they do not clearly drive the answer

### 2

- the report clearly frames the true objective
- subquestions are relevant and drive the later analysis

## Dimension 2: Coverage breadth

### What this measures

Whether the report covers the key angles needed for the task type.

### Look for

- relevant dimensions are included
- obvious important angles are not missing
- coverage is selective but sufficient

### Example dimensions by task

- company: product, competition, financials, risks, strategic change
- market: size, drivers, structure, competition, barriers, outlook
- vendor selection: capability, pricing, implementation, risk, lock-in, fit
- technical feasibility: approaches, bottlenecks, trade-offs, validation needs

### 0

- major required dimensions are missing

### 1

- some key dimensions are covered, but important ones are thin or absent

### 2

- the important dimensions are covered with clear prioritization

## Dimension 3: Evidence depth

### What this measures

Whether the report uses strong, relevant, and sufficiently independent evidence.

### Look for

- primary sources where appropriate
- independent corroboration for key claims
- current-state verification where relevant
- source diversity rather than repeated citation chains

### 0

- weak sourcing, repeated summaries, little primary support

### 1

- some good evidence exists, but key claims remain weakly supported or unevenly sourced

### 2

- key claims are supported by strong and appropriately layered evidence

## Dimension 4: Analytical depth

### What this measures

Whether the report explains meaning, trade-offs, and implications rather than merely restating facts.

### Look for

- trade-off analysis
- causal reasoning
- separation of fact, inference, and uncertainty
- explicit explanation of why findings matter

### 0

- mostly descriptive summary
- little reasoning beyond listing facts

### 1

- some analysis exists, but it remains surface-level or inconsistent

### 2

- the report clearly reasons from evidence to meaningful judgment

## Dimension 5: Counter-evidence depth

### What this measures

Whether the report seriously tests its own working thesis.

### Look for

- meaningful counter-evidence
- alternative explanations
- limitations and edge cases
- confidence adjusted based on unresolved negatives

### 0

- little or no real counter-evidence

### 1

- counter-evidence exists but is weak, token, or not integrated into judgment

### 2

- counter-evidence materially strengthens the honesty and calibration of the conclusion

## Dimension 6: Decision usefulness

### What this measures

Whether the report helps the reader decide, compare, prioritize, or act.

### Look for

- clear bottom line
- what matters most
- explicit uncertainty
- practical next steps or what to verify next

### 0

- informative but not very usable for action or judgment

### 1

- some recommendation or bottom line exists, but it is not sharp or actionable enough

### 2

- the report is clearly useful for decision-making

## How to use this rubric

After reviewing a report:

1. score each dimension from 0 to 2
2. write one short note explaining the score
3. identify the weakest two dimensions
4. decide whether the problem is:
   - a one-off execution miss
   - a repeatable failure mode
   - a gap in the skill or references
5. if repeatable, turn it into a new eval or rule update

## Interpreting patterns

### High factual accuracy, low depth

Likely symptoms:

- current facts are mostly right
- report still feels generic
- weak prioritization
- little counter-evidence
- too much summary, not enough judgment

### Good breadth, weak evidence

Likely symptoms:

- many angles covered
- sourcing is thin or repetitive
- key conclusions still fragile

### Good evidence, weak decision usefulness

Likely symptoms:

- lots of sourced information
- unclear bottom line
- user still does not know what to do

## Final discipline

Before calling a report "deep," ask:

- Did it frame the real problem?
- Did it cover the key angles?
- Did it use strong evidence?
- Did it reason, not just summarize?
- Did it test itself against counter-evidence?
- Did it help the user decide something?

If several of these are weak, the report may be polished but not truly deep.
